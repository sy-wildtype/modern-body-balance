Why Hydration Matters

Water affects your energy, focus, and overall health. Even mild dehydration can leave you tired or foggy. Here are easy ways to drink more water and feel your best.

Water helps carry nutrients to your cells, flush waste, regulate body temperature, and cushion joints. When you're low on fluids, your heart may work harder, your concentration can dip, and you might feel headaches or sluggishness. Staying hydrated is one of the simplest ways to support your body every day.

Keep Water Handy

Keep a reusable bottle on your desk, in your bag, or in the car. When it's visible, you're more likely to sip throughout the day.

Choose a bottle you like and that's easy to carry. Some people find that marked bottles or apps that remind them to drink help until the habit sticks. Filling your bottle at the same time each day—for example, when you make coffee or start work—can make hydration part of your routine.

Start and End With Water

Drink a glass when you wake up and one before bed. It's an easy routine that adds two glasses to your day.

Morning water can help rehydrate you after sleep and kick-start your digestion. A small glass before bed is fine for most people; if you find it wakes you up for bathroom trips, have your last big drink a bit earlier in the evening.

Add Flavor Without Sugar

If plain water feels boring, try a slice of lemon, cucumber, or mint. Herbal tea counts toward your fluid intake too.

Infused water can be made in a pitcher and kept in the fridge. Unsweetened sparkling water is another option if you like a little fizz. Just watch out for drinks that are high in sugar or caffeine—they're not ideal as your main hydration source.

Eat Water-Rich Foods

Fruits and vegetables like watermelon, cucumber, and oranges contribute to hydration. Include them in meals and snacks.

Soups, smoothies, and oatmeal also add fluids. In hot weather or when you're more active, you need more water, so drink before you feel thirsty and a bit extra when you sweat. Urine that's pale yellow usually means you're well hydrated; dark yellow may mean you need more fluids.

How Much Is Enough?

General guidelines suggest around 8 glasses (about 2 liters) per day, but needs vary with size, activity, and climate. Thirst is a good cue—drink when you're thirsty and a bit more when it's hot or you're exercising. If you're unsure, start with one extra glass per day and adjust from there.

Small Steps Add Up

Set a small goal—like one extra glass at lunch—and build from there. Your body will thank you.
